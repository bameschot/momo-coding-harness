# Skill: Storytelling and Narrative Writing

## Core Story Structure

- Every story needs: a character who wants something, an obstacle preventing it, and a change by the end.
- The inciting incident disrupts the character's ordinary world and forces them into the story. It should arrive early — by the end of the first act.
- The midpoint is not just a plot beat — it is where the character shifts from reactive to active. Before the midpoint they respond; after it, they pursue.
- The climax resolves the central conflict. It should feel both inevitable and surprising: earned by everything that came before, yet not telegraphed.
- Endings must answer the question the story raised. Do not open new questions in the final pages unless they are deliberate and thematically resonant.

## Character

- Characters are defined by what they do under pressure, not by description. Show decision-making in difficult moments.
- Every significant character wants something — even if it is only a glass of water. Know what each character wants in each scene.
- Distinguish between a character's stated goal (what they say they want) and their underlying need (what would actually make them whole). The story is often the gap between these two things.
- Give characters contradictions: brave but selfish, kind but dishonest. Flat characters have one trait; real characters have conflicting ones.
- Change is not mandatory — a character who refuses to change while the world demands it is a valid and powerful arc.

## Scene Construction

- Every scene should do at least two of these three things: advance the plot, reveal character, establish theme.
- Enter scenes as late as possible; leave as early as possible. Cut everything before the first interesting thing and everything after the last.
- Conflict in a scene does not have to be confrontational — it can be a character wanting to leave and being unable to, or wanting to say something and choosing not to.
- End scenes with a turn: something the reader (or character) did not expect, or something that changes what the next scene must be.

## Prose Style

- Prefer concrete, specific detail over abstract description. "A rusted iron gate" beats "an old entrance."
- Use active voice as the default. Passive voice when the subject is unknown, unimportant, or deliberately withheld.
- Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences hit hard. Longer sentences build rhythm, carry the reader through a sequence of images or ideas, and slow the pace when you want contemplation.
- Dialogue should feel natural but be more purposeful than real speech. Cut all the small talk that does not reveal character or advance something.
- Show interiority sparingly. A single well-chosen detail of physical sensation or a brief thought can do more than a paragraph of explained feeling.

## Tone and Voice

- Voice is the personality on the page. It comes from word choice, sentence rhythm, what the narrator notices and what they ignore.
- Tone should match subject: do not undercut genuine grief with irony, do not over-dramatise something small.
- Consistency matters more than perfection. A reader can adapt to an unusual voice; they cannot adapt to a voice that changes arbitrarily.

## Revision Guidance

- First draft: get it down. Second draft: get it right. Do not edit while drafting.
- Cut ruthlessly: if a scene, paragraph, or sentence does not earn its place, remove it. Readers do not miss what was never there.
- Read dialogue aloud. If you hesitate or stumble, rewrite it.
- Look for scenes where nothing is at stake. Add stakes or cut the scene.
