# Skill: Storytelling and Narrative Writing

## Core rules

- Every story needs a character who WANTS something, an OBSTACLE, and a CHANGE by the end.
- Reveal character through action under pressure, not description.
- Enter scenes late, leave early — cut everything before the first interesting thing and after the last.
- Use concrete, specific detail ("a rusted iron gate"), not abstraction ("an old entrance").
- Default to active voice. Vary sentence length deliberately for rhythm.
- Cut anything that does not earn its place. Readers don't miss what was never there.

## Structure

- The inciting incident (disrupts the ordinary world) arrives early — by the end of act one.
- At the midpoint the character shifts from reactive to active.
- The climax resolves the central conflict; it should feel inevitable yet surprising.
- The ending answers the question the story raised. Don't open new questions at the end.

## Character

- Every character wants something in every scene — even just a glass of water.
- Distinguish the stated goal (what they say they want) from the real need. The gap is often the story.
- Give characters contradictions (brave but selfish). Flat characters have one trait.

## Scene

- Each scene should do at least two of: advance plot, reveal character, establish theme.
- Conflict need not be confrontation — wanting to leave and being unable to is conflict.
- End scenes on a turn: something that changes what the next scene must be.

## Dialogue

- Make it more purposeful than real speech. Cut small talk that reveals nothing.
- People talk around what they fear to say — let subtext carry the emotion.
- Use `said`/`asked` (invisible) or action beats. Give each character a distinct voice.

## Point of view

- Pick one POV per scene and stay in it. You can only reveal what that character can know or infer.
- First person: intimate, subjective. Third limited: most versatile. Omniscient: powerful but risks head-hopping.

## Revision

- First draft: get it down. Second: get it right. Don't edit while drafting.
- Read dialogue aloud; rewrite anything you stumble over.
- Find scenes with nothing at stake — add stakes or cut them.
